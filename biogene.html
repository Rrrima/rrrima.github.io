BIOGENE DIVERSITY

Soil microorganisms are important members of forest ecosystems. The spatial distribution pattern of soil microbes along with elevation was an important topic of microbial ecology research. In this study, we used 16S rRNA gene pyrosequencing methods to determine diversities and structures of bacterial communities and their functions, which may have potential effects on GHGs emissions, to examine whether elevation led to variable patterns of bacterial diversity in soils of the Qinling Mountains. The results showed that the bacterial communities diversities and structures were decided by elevation to some extent.

Motovation

The elevational gradient in plant and animal diversity is one of the most widely documented patterns in ecology and, researches on elevational diversity gradients had focused almost exclusively on plant and animal taxa. As a result, we do not know whether microbes exhibit elevational gradients in diversity that parallel those observed for macroscopic taxa (Fierer et al. 2011). This means a key knowledge gap in ecology, especially given the ubiquity, abundance, and functional importance of microbes.
The Qinling Mountains are a major east-west mountain ranges in the middle part of China. With a high forest coverage rate, they may play an important role in relieving global warming. And, the interaction between species and the environment of the Qinling Mountains forms unique habitat environments along with elevational gradient, which also leads to the formation of unique microbial diversity and structural features in the soil.





Data Preparation

[sampling]: The study area is located in south side of the Qinling Mountains, Shaanxi Province, China. The 100 m×100 m field site was set in all three sampling sites, and in each field site, 5 sampling plots were determined to obtain soil samples and GHGs samples.
[DNA Extraction]: Total genome DNA from samples was extracted using CTAB/SDS method.
[Pyrosequencing Data Treatment]: Sequences with ≥97% similarity were assigned to the same OTUs. For each representative sequence, the GreenGene Database was used based on RDP classifier (Version 2.2) algorithm to annotate taxonomic information.sented to both researchers and users with a 24 hour interval. Users will be paid at the end of studies.




Statistical Analysis

Statistics analysis of OTUs richness via rarefaction, Coverage, Chao1 and Shannon’s indices were performed using QIIME (Version 2.0) and displayed with R software (Version 3.4.4). Cluster analysis was preceded by Weighted Unifrac distance using vegan package in R software. To get better insight into the dissimilarity of soil bacterial communities among the 3 soil types, a Venn diagram of shared and unique OTUs was performed also using R software. Furthermore, detailed analyses were performed to visualize abundance of communities with heatmap and the relationships between communities diversity and environment factors with RDA using the vegan, pheatmap, and ggplot2 package in R software.





Results

The results showed that 48 phyla and some unidentified bacteria named unclassified were detected in this research (Table.S1). And there were some differences due to dominant phyla among the three groups. Sequences affiliated with Proteobacteria, Acidobacteria, Actinobacteria, Verrucomicrobia were common in A and B groups, while the four most abundant phylum in C group were Proteobacteria, Acidobacteria, Verrucomicrobia, and Nitrospirae. It’s well known that phylum Nitrospirae was closely related to nitrogen cycle of soil.







To get better understanding about the differences of soil bacterial community among the three soil sites, heatmap analyses of the most abundant 50 genus were applied, which highlighted their relative distributions and abundances. As showed in heatmap diagram, the abundance of dominant 50 genus differed among the three soil types. The dominant genus in each soil type were also different. For instant, A group was dominated by genus Sphingomonas and genus Massilia, whereas B group was dominated by genus Candidatus Solibacter and genus Bryobacter, C group was dominated by genus Nitrospira.

The results about CO2 emissions from three soils types showed a similar pattern with abundance of microbial functions on CO2 emissions, CO2 emissions of A (0.30 ± 0.03 g·m-2·h-1) group were higher than B (0.27± 0.08 g·m-2·h-1) and C (0.19± 0.09 g·m-2·h-1) group. The results of N2O emissions from three soils types also showed the same patterns with abundance of microbial functions. Differ from abundance of microbial functions on CO2 and N2O emissions, and which had relationships with elevation to some extent, abundance of microbial functions on CH4 emissions was much lower. This leads to CH4 absorption occurred in research region.



We use redundancy analysis to show correlations between the bacterial communities and geochemical properties of the 3 soil groups. The arrows represent geochemical factors measured. Among the selected geochemical factors, elevation (p < 0.05), temperature (p < 0.01) and precipitation (p < 0.01) were important geochemical factors that correlated with the soil bacterial community composition in this region. However, pH was not significantly correlated with soil bacteria community composition (Table.2), which was different to the results of Griffiths et al. (2011).




The results about CO2 emissions from three soils types showed a similar pattern with abundance of microbial functions on CO2 emissions, CO2 emissions of A (0.30 ± 0.03 g·m-2·h-1) group were higher than B (0.27± 0.08 g·m-2·h-1) and C (0.19± 0.09 g·m-2·h-1) group. The results of N2O emissions from three soils types also showed the same patterns with abundance of microbial functions. Differ from abundance of microbial functions on CO2 and N2O emissions, and which had relationships with elevation to some extent, abundance of microbial functions on CH4 emissions was much lower. This leads to CH4 absorption occurred in research region.
